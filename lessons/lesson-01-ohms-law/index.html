The Golden Rule of DC Electricity

🔬 What Is Electricity, Really?

Everything around you — this page, a wire, your hand — is made of incredibly tiny building blocks called atoms. You can't see them. Millions of them fit on the head of a pin.

Inside every atom are even smaller particles. One kind is called an electron. In some materials — especially metals like copper — electrons aren't locked in place. They're loose enough to move.

When electrons move through a wire in the same direction, that movement is electricity. That's it. Electricity isn't magic — it's electrons on the move.

Now here's the important part: that movement doesn't just happen on its own. Something has to push the electrons. Something can slow them down. And the result is how many actually get through. Those three things have names.

👥 Three Things That Are Always in the Room

Every time electricity flows, three things are happening at the same time. We give them names so we can talk about them.

Volt — The Push

Volt is the force that makes electrons move. Without Volt, the electrons just sit there. Nothing happens. Think of Volt as the reason the electrons go anywhere at all.

Ohm — The Squeeze

Ohm is resistance — how hard the path fights back against the electrons trying to move through it. A thin wire has more Ohm than a thick one. More Ohm means fewer electrons get through.

Amp — The Flow

Amp is how many electrons are actually moving through the wire right now. It's not a guess — it's the result of whatever Volt is pushing and whatever Ohm is resisting. Amp tells you what's really happening.

⚖️ The Golden Rule

These three are always connected. If you know any two, you can figure out the third. That connection is called Ohm's Law:

Volts ÷ Ohms = Amps
Push ÷ Resistance = Flow

More push with the same resistance — more flow.
More resistance with the same push — less flow.
The math doesn't lie. Amp is always the honest result.

📝 Quick Review
  • Volt is the push. No Volt — nothing moves.
  • Ohm is the squeeze. More Ohm means less gets through.
  • Amp is the flow. It's what you actually get.
